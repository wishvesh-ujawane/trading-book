## **1. Introduction — Why Most Breakout Trades Fail** 

This session addresses one of the most critical topics for traders: understanding when to wait after a breakout or breakdown, and when not to. Balakrishna opens with a crucial insight — the majority of traders who take breakout and breakdown trades end up failing, and this session explains exactly why. 

## **1.1 The Core Problem** 

- 99% of traders expect the market to move in a straight line after they enter a trade. 

- When they buy, they expect the price to keep rising without stopping. 

- When they sell, they expect the price to keep falling without pausing. 

- This expectation is unrealistic — markets never move in straight lines. 

**Key Insight:** The market always moves in a stop-and-go pattern. It expands, then pauses, then expands again. Both phases — movement and rest — are essential and complement each other. 

**📌 Note:** _Think of it like a long road trip: you travel for a while, take a break to rest and refuel, then continue. The rest is not wasted time — it gives you the energy to complete the journey. Markets behave the same way._

## **2.1 NRC — Narrow Range Consolidation (Price Blast Structure)** 

## **What is NRC?** 

- Price is stuck in a very small, tight range for an extended period. 

- It is neither moving up nor down — it is compressed. 

- During this time, price is NOT spending energy; instead, it is storing it. 

- This structure is also called the Price Blast Structure because of what happens after. 

## **The Analogy — Kidnapped in a 3x3 Room** 

Imagine being kidnapped and locked in a tiny 3x3 room for 5 days. You cannot sleep comfortably, you cannot walk, you cannot run. Your energy is completely suppressed. On the sixth day, the door opens. What do you do? You run with full force — you blast out. 

This is exactly how NRC behaves. Price is compressed for a long time, storing energy. The moment a breakout or breakdown occurs, price blasts out with full force — without giving any pullback.

**Critical Rule:** After a breakout from NRC, price will NOT give a pullback. Do not wait for a pullback to enter — you will miss the move entirely. 

**How to Identify NRC on a Chart** 

- Look for a large candle — this is the Mother Candle. 

- After the Mother Candle, observe the subsequent candles (called Baby Candles). 

- In a valid NRC, the maximum body of most baby candles should be contained within the range of the Mother Candle. 

- Count the baby candles — the more, the stronger the NRC (e.g., 17 candles inside the mother candle's range is a very strong NRC). 

- Mark the Highest High and Lowest Low of all candles in the NRC zone. 

- These two lines form the NRC boundary — the breakout zone to watch. 

**📌 Note:** _Volume confirmation: While volume is not mandatory, if the breakout candle shows higher volume than the preceding candles inside the NRC, it adds confirmation to the trade._ 

## **Trading Rules for NRC** 

- NEVER trade inside the NRC. Price is not moving — there is no trade opportunity. 

- Wait for a breakout (above the highest high) or a breakdown (below the lowest low). 

- Once the breakout happens, take the trade in the direction of the breakout. 

- Do not expect a pullback — take the trade at the breakout level. 

- The post-breakout move is fast and sharp, with no retracement. 

## **2.2 WRC — Wide Range Consolidation (Price Rotation Structure)** 

## **What is WRC?** 

- Price is moving in a wide range — oscillating between a defined support and resistance level. 

- Unlike NRC, price here IS moving — it is traveling from one boundary to the other. 

- Because price is active and moving, it is spending energy. 

- This is also called the Price Rotation Structure because price rotates between two levels. 

## **The Analogy — Comfortable House Under Confinement** 

Imagine being confined in a large, comfortable house with food, TV, and space to move around. You can eat, walk, and enjoy yourself. When someone opens the front door, you don't have the same desperate urge to run out — you are comfortable. You may step out briefly, then come back. 

This is how WRC works. Price has space, it is moving, and it keeps spending its energy. When it reaches the resistance or support boundary, it tries to break out — but it is exhausted. It fails and returns back inside the range.

**Critical Rule:** In a WRC, 85% of breakout and breakdown attempts will FAIL. This is the high-probability trade — fading the breakout. 

## **Why Do WRC Breakouts Fail?** 

- Price has been traveling inside the WRC, constantly spending energy. 

- By the time price reaches resistance to attempt a breakout, it has used up most of its energy. 

- To break through a resistance level, extra energy is required. 

- Since there is no extra energy available, the breakout fails and price reverses. 

- Same logic applies for breakdowns — price arrives at support exhausted and reverses back up. 

**Trading the WRC — Multiple Trade Opportunities** 

- Inside the WRC, you CAN take trades as price oscillates between support and resistance. 

- Look for Breakout Failure at the resistance — short the market when the breakout fails. 

- Look for Breakdown Failure at the support — buy the market when the breakdown fails. 

- This rotation can happen multiple times within the same WRC, providing multiple profitable trades. 

**📌 Note:** _The WRC is excellent for range trading. You make money on both sides — buying near support (breakdown failure) and selling near resistance (breakout failure)._

## **4. Price Expansion, Origin, and WRC Trading in Action** 

Understanding the concept of Price Expansion and the Origin of a move is essential for trading the WRC effectively. 

## **4.1 Price Expansion** 

- When market halts (consolidates), it generates energy. 

- Once sufficient energy is gathered, the market expands — it moves powerfully in one direction. 

- Example: Price moves from 1 rupee to 50 rupees with a strong move. It then halts, gathers energy, and expands from 50 rupees to 120 rupees without stopping. This continuous move is called Price Expansion. 

## **4.2 The Concept of Origin** 

Every significant price move (expansion) has an Origin — the starting point of that move. Identifying the origin is a key skill in the WRC trading strategy. 

- When price makes a big move upward (from 50 to 120), the Origin of that high (120) is the starting point of the move (50). 

- When price makes a big move downward (from 120 to 48), the Origin of that low (48) is 120. 

- Draw a horizontal support or resistance line at the Origin level. 

- When price returns to the Origin and attempts to break through it, it will likely fail — this is your trade entry point. 

**📌 Note:** _The Origin concept is the backbone of WRC trading. The Origin of the high becomes resistance, and the Origin of the low becomes support. These are your trading zones._ 

## **4.3 Step-by-Step WRC Trading Example** 

Here is how a full WRC trading cycle plays out: 

- Step 1: Price is at 1 rupee. It consolidates and then expands to 50 rupees. 

- Step 2: From 50 rupees, price expands strongly to 120 rupees (Price Expansion). The Origin of this move is 50. 

- Step 3: Price is now at 120. It tries to break out above 120 — but fails (WRC breakout failure). Draw resistance at 120. 

- Step 4: Price falls from 120 back towards 50 (the Origin). Draw a support line at 50. 

- Step 5: Price reaches 50, tries to break down — fails (WRC breakdown failure). This is a BUY opportunity. 

- Step 6: Price reverses upward. The Origin of this low (50) was 120. Mark resistance again at 120.

**Trading the Failure:** You are not trading the breakout — you are trading the FAILURE of the breakout. This is the edge. 85% of breakouts from a WRC fail. 

## **5. Using Order Flow for Trade Entry** 

Balakrishna briefly introduced the concept of using Order Flow as a precision tool for entries within the WRC strategy. 

## **5.1 What is BMC?** 

- BMC (Bullish Market Candle or Buying Market Candle — precise term to be confirmed in advanced sessions) is a pattern used for entry confirmation. 

- BMC should be identified at or near the support level within the WRC. 

- The process: Mark the High and Low of the BMC pattern. When the High of the BMC is broken, take the Buy trade. 

- This gives a very precise entry near the bottom of the WRC range — capturing the move from support to resistance.
