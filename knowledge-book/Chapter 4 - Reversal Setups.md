## **1. NRC Identification in Real Time (Live Chart)** 

One of the most common student questions is: how do I identify NRC and WRC on a live running chart, especially right at market open? Balakrishna provides a clear, step-by-step explanation. 

## **1.1 Starting from Market Open — 9:30 AM** 

- When the market opens, the very first candle that forms is observed closely. 

- When the second candle forms, you already have what you need to identify an NRC. 

- One Mother Candle + One Baby Candle = a valid NRC structure. You only need two candles. 

## **1.2 Step-by-Step Real-Time NRC Identification** 

- Step 1: The first candle forms. This becomes your Mother Candle. 

- Step 2: The second candle forms. Check: is the maximum body of this baby candle inside the range of the Mother Candle? If yes — you have an NRC. 

- Step 3: As each subsequent candle forms, check whether its maximum body remains inside the Mother Candle's range. 

- Step 4: As long as candles keep forming inside the Mother Candle range, the NRC continues to be valid. It keeps building. 

- Step 5: Once a candle clearly breaks outside the Mother Candle's range — that is the breakdown or breakout. The NRC has triggered. 

- Step 6: Mark the Highest High and Lowest Low of all candles within the NRC zone and extend these lines forward. 

## **1.3 What to Do After Marking the NRC** 

- Extend the NRC boundary lines (highest high and lowest low) forward in time. 

- Wait — do not trade inside the NRC. Simply mark it and stay patient. 

- When the breakout/breakdown candle forms, that is your trade trigger (after learning the specific setups in later sessions). 

**📌 Note:** _Volume does NOT matter for NRC identification. The only things that matter are: the Mother Candle's range, and whether baby candle bodies remain within that range. Do not over-complicate it._

## **2. Block Size in Order Flow — Understanding and Setup** 

Block size determines the granularity of data you see in order flow candles. Getting the right block size is essential for reading order flow clearly and comfortably. 

## **2.1 What is Block Size?** 

Each candle on your order flow chart is made up of individual 'blocks', just like a wall is made of individual bricks. The block size determines how much price movement each brick represents. 

- If block size is ₹1: every ₹1 of price movement creates one block. Candles become very tall and detailed — often too tall to read on a single screen. 

- If block size is ₹5: every ₹5 of price movement creates one block. Five times fewer blocks — candle becomes shorter and more readable. 

- If block size is ₹50: every ₹50 creates one block. Very compact candles — you can see many candles at once, but less granularity. 

**Shopkeeper Analogy:** A shopkeeper is asked to report every minute. It is overwhelming and impractical. Instead, he reports every hour — one number representing the entire hour's business. Increasing block size is like increasing the reporting interval: less detail, but far easier to manage and interpret. 

## **2.2 The Block Size Formula** 

Every instrument has a different Tick Size — the minimum price increment it can move. The Block Size formula lets you convert your desired block size into the Value field in the order flow software.

## **Value** 

The number you input into the order flow software's block size/value field.

## **2.3 What is Tick Size?** 

- Tick size is the minimum permissible price increment for any instrument. 

- Analogy: when buying bananas, you buy in multiples of 1 banana. You cannot buy half a banana. 1 is the tick size.

- Each stock and instrument has its own tick size — find it in the order flow software settings under the instrument details. 

- Tick size may change over time based on exchange regulations. When it changes, recalculate your block size value.

- Multiple candles are visible on your screen simultaneously.

## **5. Drawing Support and Resistance on a Live Chart** 

This section gives the exact step-by-step method for drawing support and resistance lines while the market is live — one of the most practical skills in this session. 

## **5.1 Starting Point — Find the LTP** 

- LTP = Last Traded Price = Current Market Price. This is where the market is right now. 

- All your reference lines are drawn in relation to the LTP. You need support below LTP and resistance above LTP. 

- You do NOT need minor supports or resistances — only major ones. 

## **5.2 Drawing Support Levels (Below LTP)** 

- Step 1: Look below the current LTP. Scan the chart to the left for swing lows (local price bottoms). 

- Step 2: For each swing low, ask: 'Was there a big move upward from here?' If yes → Major Support. Mark it. 

- Step 3: Look for the origin of the current LTP. Which major swing low is the source of the rally that brought price to its current level? This is the most important support level. 

- Step 4: Use the Magnet tool (horizontal line with magnet snap) to draw the horizontal line precisely at the lowest point of the swing low candle. Click once — the line snaps to the exact low. 

- Step 5: Continue looking further below for additional major swing lows. Draw lines for each — you may have Support 1, Support 2, Support 3 below the current LTP. 

## **5.3 Drawing Resistance Levels (Above LTP)** 

- Step 1: Look above the current LTP. Scan to the left for swing highs (local price peaks). 

- Step 2: For each swing high, ask: 'Was there a big move downward from here?' If yes → Major Resistance. Mark it. 

- Step 3: Find the nearest peak above the LTP first — this is the most immediately relevant resistance. 

- Step 4: Use the horizontal line tool, touch the tip of the swing high candle, and click to draw. The line is drawn at the exact swing high. 

- Step 5: Continue looking further above for additional swing highs. Draw Resistance 1, Resistance 2, Resistance 3 above LTP.

## **6. Flip Areas — Change in Polarity** 

Flip Areas (also called Change in Polarity or CIP) are one of the most powerful and reliable reference levels in technical analysis. They describe what happens when price breaks through a support or resistance level. 

## **6.1 The Core Concept** 

- When a SUPPORT level is broken: that support level flips and now acts as RESISTANCE. 

- When a RESISTANCE level is broken: that resistance level flips and now acts as SUPPORT. 

- • This flip is predictable and repeatable. If you know where the flip zone is, you can anticipate where price will react next. 

## **6.2 Why Does the Flip Happen?** 

The psychological mechanism is straightforward: 

- When price was supported at a level, buyers entered there. They had a positive experience — price went up from their buy. 

- Now price breaks back down through that support. Those buyers are now sitting at breakeven or at a loss. 

- When price rallies back up to the old support level, those buyers will SELL to exit (at breakeven) or the market will feel resistance from previous sellers who originally held that level. 

- This creates resistance exactly where support used to be — the flip. 

**College Crush Analogy:** You loved a girl in college. She cheated on you and left. Now if she comes back, you are angry at her. The same person who gave you positive feelings before now causes negative feelings — a complete flip in polarity. Price reacts the same way at old support/resistance levels. 

**Floor and Ceiling Analogy:** When you are on the first floor, the floor is your support and the ceiling is your resistance. When you go up to the second floor, the old ceiling of the first floor becomes the new floor (support) of the second floor. The next ceiling above becomes the new resistance.

## **7. Multi-Timeframe Analysis Rules** 

Many traders confuse themselves with multi-timeframe analysis. Balakrishna provides clear, simple rules that eliminate confusion. 

## **7.1 The Golden Rule** 

|||
|---|---|
|**Action**|**Timeframe**|
|||
|||
|**Draw Support & Resistance**|HIGHER Timeframe (e.g., 15 minutes)|
|||
|||
|**Execute the Trade**|LOWER Timeframe (e.g., 5 minutes)|
|||



- Never draw support and resistance on the lower timeframe and trade on it. 

- Never draw support and resistance on the higher timeframe and then try to trade directly on the higher timeframe. 

## **7.2 The Gap Between Timeframes** 

- The two timeframes you use must have a minimum gap of 3 times. 

- For example: If your lower trading timeframe is 5 minutes, the minimum higher timeframe for drawing S/R is 15 minutes (5 × 3 = 15). Good. 

- Another valid pair: 15 minutes (drawing) and 5 minutes (trading). ✓ 

- Another valid pair: 75 minutes (drawing) and 15 minutes (trading). ✓ 

- An INVALID pair: 1 hour (drawing) and 5 minutes (trading). The gap is 12× — too large, creates confusion. 

- Another INVALID pair: 15 minutes (drawing) and 10 minutes (trading). The gap is only 1.5× — too small. 

**Recommended Combination:** Draw on 15-minute chart. Execute/trade on 5-minute chart. This is the standard combination for intraday trading in this system. 

## **7.3 Why Not Use Many Timeframes?** 

- Multi-timeframe analysis with 3, 4, or 5 timeframes creates enormous confusion. 

- Different timeframes will show conflicting signals — one says buy, another says sell. 

- The more timeframes you use, the more time you spend resolving conflicts instead of trading. 

- Two timeframes are sufficient. Keep it simple. Maximum effectiveness with minimum complexity.

## **8. Order Flow at Reference Levels** 

Order flow is NOT applied randomly across the chart. It has one specific purpose in this system: to confirm entries at reference levels. 

## **8.1 The Role of Order Flow** 

- References tell you WHERE to look for trades. Order flow tells you WHEN to enter. 

- You wait for price to arrive at a major support or resistance level. 

- Once price is at the reference level, you activate order flow analysis to look for confirmation of a trade entry. 

- Without a reference level, there is no point reading order flow. Order flow without context is noise. 

**Key Principle:** Order flow should be seen ONLY at the references. It is not a standalone tool — it works in conjunction with the support/resistance framework you have built. 

## **8.2 Options Trading — A Critical Rule** 

- Order flow is NEVER applied directly to options charts. 

- Options pricing is driven by multiple variables (Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega — the Greeks), not just price. Technical analysis and order flow do not apply directly. 

- You ALWAYS analyse the Futures chart (Nifty Futures, Bank Nifty Futures) using order flow and support/resistance. 

- Based on your analysis of the futures chart, you then execute your trade in the options market. 

- The futures chart is your analysis instrument. The options market is your execution vehicle.
