- The imbalance is expressed as a ratio/percentage: if buy volume is 3x the sell volume, that is a 300% imbalance.

## **2. Order Types: Limit Orders and Market Orders** 

There are two fundamental ways to place an order in the market. Understanding the difference between them is the foundation of order flow analysis. 

## **2.1 Limit Order (Passive Order)** 

A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell only at a specific price you choose. The order sits in the market waiting until the market price reaches your desired level. 

## **Key Characteristics** 

- You choose the exact price at which the trade executes. 

- The order will NOT execute until the market price reaches your specified level. 

- Also called a Passive Order because it waits patiently — it does not move the market. 

- Types: Limit Buy Order (buy at a lower price) and Limit Sell Order (sell at a higher price). 

## **Example — Limit Sell Order** 

- You bought Nifty futures at ₹24,000. Price is currently at ₹25,000 (₹1,000 profit). 

- You want to sell at ₹25,100. You place a Limit Sell Order at ₹25,100. 

- The order sits waiting. It will only execute when price rises from ₹25,000 to ₹25,100. 

- • If price never reaches ₹25,100, the order remains unfilled. 

## **Example — Limit Buy Order** 

- Price is at ₹25,000 and you want to buy at ₹24,000 (expecting a pullback). 

- You place a Limit Buy Order at ₹24,000. 

- The order waits until price falls to ₹24,000, then executes automatically. 

**Analogy:** Person A is told to buy 1 kg cucumber at exactly ₹50. He will not buy at ₹60 or ₹70. He waits until he finds a shop with the exact price. That is a limit order — disciplined, fixed, passive. 

## **2.2 Market Order (Aggressive Order)** 

A market order is an instruction to buy or sell immediately at whatever price is currently available. You give up control of the price in exchange for immediate execution. 

## **Key Characteristics**

- Executes immediately at the best available price in the market. 

- You have no control over the execution price. 

- Also called an Aggressive Order because it is proactive — it hunts for the nearest available counterparty. 

- Types: Market Buy Order and Market Sell Order. 

- Market orders MOVE the market. They shift the Last Transaction Price (LTP). 

## **Example — Market Sell Order** 

- Price is at ₹25,000, you want to sell immediately. 

- You place a Market Sell Order. It executes right away at the best bid available — immediately. 

- • You do not wait for any specific price level. 

**Analogy:** Person B is told to buy cucumber at any cost — no price restriction. He walks into the first shop and buys at ₹100, ₹200, or whatever the price is. He does not bargain or wait. That is a market order — aggressive and immediate.

This is one of the most important concepts in order flow. Every single transaction in the market requires BOTH a limit order and a market order to come together. They are complementary — like husband and wife.

## **3. How Market Orders Move Price — LTP Concept** 

Understanding how price actually moves up and down is critical for reading order flow. The key concept here is the LTP — Last Transaction Price. 

## **3.1 What is LTP?** 

- LTP = Last Transaction Price. It is the price at which the most recent trade was executed. 

- The LTP is what you see as the 'current market price' on your screen. 

- Every time a new trade executes, the LTP shifts to the price of that new trade. 

## **3.2 How Price Rises (Uptrend)** 

- A market buyer places a Market Buy Order. The software looks for the nearest Limit Sell Order above the current LTP. 

- The trade executes at that Limit Sell price. This becomes the new LTP — higher than before. 

- The next Market Buy Order executes at the next available Limit Sell — even higher. LTP shifts up again. 

- As market buy orders keep flowing in, LTP keeps shifting higher and higher, creating an upward price movement. 

**Cause of Uptrend:** Repeated market buying activity. Whenever demand (market buy orders) exceeds available supply at current levels, price is pushed higher. 

## **3.3 How Price Falls (Downtrend)** 

- A market seller places a Market Sell Order. The software looks for the nearest Limit Buy Order below the current LTP. 

- The trade executes at that Limit Buy price. LTP shifts lower. 

- Repeated market selling forces the LTP lower and lower. 

- To sell at the market, you must offer at a discount — below the last transaction price. This pulls price down. 

**Cause of Downtrend:** Repeated market selling activity. Supply exceeding demand forces price to fall as sellers must undercut the LTP to find buyers. 

## **4. Law of Gravity** 

The Law of Gravity is a fundamental concept that explains why price slides down even when there are no active sellers. This concept directly feeds into understanding what a Pullback is.

## **4.1 The Principle** 

- When price has been moving upward due to strong buying, it reaches a level where no new buyers are willing to pay higher prices. 

- Buying activity stops. The smart money (institutions) has finished accumulating. 

- There are also no aggressive sellers yet — no one is actively shorting the market. 

- In the absence of new buying, price naturally starts to slide down slowly. This is the Law of Gravity. 

- Even without active sellers pushing price down, the market will drift lower simply because no one is lifting it higher. 

**Key Insight:** You do not need active sellers for price to fall. You only need the absence of buyers. Just as gravity pulls objects down without any active force pushing them, the market falls without sellers when buying interest disappears. 

## **4.2 Why This Happens — Practical Example** 

- Gold was rising strongly. Everyone was buying every day, joining the rally. 

- Smart money finishes buying and stops. No more large-scale buying demand exists. 

- Retail traders who bought near the top expect the market to continue rising — but it stops. 

- After 2-3 candles of sideways movement, retail traders start to exit at market prices (not selling short — just exiting long positions). 

- This exit selling (at whatever price is available) gradually pushes price lower, slowly and steadily. 

- Eventually, new buyers appear at discounted levels, attracted by the lower price — and the market bounces. 

**📌 Note:** _The Law of Gravity is why pullbacks happen slowly and gradually. It is NOT an aggressive selloff — it is a quiet, gradual drift lower due to lack of demand._

## **5. Pullback vs Sell-Off** 

Two of the most frequently confused concepts in trading. Balakrishna makes a very clear distinction that every student must understand — because your trading strategy depends on which one is occurring. 

## **5.1 What is a Pullback?** 

A pullback is a temporary, mild retracement within an ongoing trend. It is NOT a reversal and NOT a sell-off. 

## **Characteristics of a Pullback** 

- Slow, gradual movement — price drifts down over many candles. 

- Low volume during the pullback — there are no aggressive sellers. 

- Caused by profit booking (participants exiting long positions at the market price) and the absence of new buyers, not by active shorting. 

- The retracement is typically less than 50% of the prior move (and in a healthy trend, less than 15% for stocks/index). 

- After the pullback completes, new buyers enter at the discounted price and the trend continues. 

- More candles are used in the pullback direction (slow), fewer candles in the trend direction (fast). 

**Rule of 15% and 50%:** In stocks and indices (not options), if price retraces more than 15% against the trend direction, be cautious — it may not be a simple pullback. Comebacks below 50% of the prior move are classified as pullbacks. 

## **Pullback Example** 

- Stock moves from ₹100 to ₹1,000 (900 points up). 

- It then dips to ₹900 (100 points down = ~11% retracement). 

- This is a valid pullback — slow, mild, and proportionally small. 

- Compare: stock moves to ₹1,000 and then drops to ₹800 (20% retracement) — this raises concern about whether it is still a pullback. 

## **5.2 What is a Sell-Off?** 

A sell-off is an aggressive, sharp fall driven by active sellers entering the market with high volume. It is fundamentally different from a pullback. 

## **Characteristics of a Sell-Off** 

- Fast, sharp, aggressive movement — price drops quickly over few candles.

## **6. Liquidity — The Foundation of Price Behaviour** 

Liquidity is one of the most important concepts in professional trading. It determines where price will stop, where it will reverse, and how deeply price can penetrate a zone. 

## **6.1 What is Liquidity?** 

- Liquidity is a location in the market where a large number of both buyers and sellers coexist simultaneously. 

- It means there is equal or near-equal supply and demand at a specific price zone. 

- In a liquid market, you can buy or sell quickly with minimal price difference between your order and the execution price. 

- In an illiquid market, large gaps exist between buyers and sellers — you may have to sell at a steep discount to find a buyer. 

**Food Street Analogy:** A food street with many shops selling the same item at similar prices (e.g., idli at ₹49-₹51) is a highly liquid area. Prices are tight, transactions happen fast. If one shop charges ₹60, buyers simply walk to the next shop. This competition keeps prices stable — just like liquidity keeps market prices in a tight range.

## **6.4 The Swimming Pool / Oil Pool Analogy** 

Balakrishna uses a brilliant analogy to explain how liquidity zones behave when price approaches them: 

- Water Pool: If you jump into a water pool from above, you dive straight through the surface quickly — low resistance. This represents a low-liquidity zone where price passes through easily. 

- Oil Pool (High Viscosity): If you jump into an oil pool, the thick viscosity slows your entry. You go in slowly, and the thickness resists your movement. This represents a HIGH-liquidity zone — like an NRC. 

- 

   - The viscosity (thickness) of the oil = the depth of liquidity at that price zone. 

- Price approaching a high-liquidity NRC zone will be rejected at the surface (strong liquidity) or will slowly be absorbed and enter before reversing.

## **6.6 Liquidity Zones and Price Reversal** 

- For price to break through a liquidity zone (e.g., an NRC acting as resistance), it must consume ALL the pending limit sell orders inside that zone. 

- Every sell order in the zone must be absorbed (bought) before price can advance further. 

- Until all the supply is consumed, price cannot go higher. It must 'kill all sellers' first. 

- This is why high-liquidity zones are such reliable support/resistance levels — they take a lot of energy to overcome.

## **7. Delta — Measuring Order Flow Dominance** 

Delta is the most important single number in order flow analysis. It reveals who is in control of each candle — buyers or sellers — and by how much. 

## **7.1 What is Delta?** 

- Delta = Buy Volume − Sell Volume 

- It measures the NET dominance of buyers over sellers (or sellers over buyers) within a specific candle or time period. 

- Delta is NOT a count of the number of buyers/sellers — it represents the excess of one side over the other. 

**Delta Value Meaning Positive Delta (e.g., +461)** Buyers are dominant. Buy volume exceeds sell volume by 461 contracts/shares. **Negative Delta (e.g., -120)** Sellers are dominant. Sell volume exceeds buy volume by 120 contracts/shares. **Delta near zero** Balance — neither buyers nor sellers are dominant. Exchange is happening equally. **Large positive delta** Strong buyer dominance — price likely to move upward. **Large negative delta** Strong seller dominance — price likely to move downward. 

## **7.2 How to Find Delta on Pro Charting** 

- In order flow view, zoom in on the chart until you can see the numbers within each candle clearly. 

- At the bottom of the chart, click on the bar statistics area (double-click on any candle). 

- A bar statistics window opens showing: Total Volume, Buy Volume, Sell Volume, and Delta. 

- To simplify: in the settings, remove Buy Volume and Sell Volume — keep only Delta value displayed. 

- Delta is displayed: Buy Volume number at top of candle, Sell Volume at bottom. The difference = Delta.

## **7.4 When Does Delta Create a Price Effect?** 

- Mild dominance (e.g., 1,000 buyers vs 1,200 sellers) — delta of 200 — minimal impact. Price will not move significantly. 

- Moderate dominance — some price movement, but not a strong trend. 

- When dominance exceeds 100% (sellers become 2x or more of buyers, or vice versa) — strong directional pressure on price. 

- At extreme delta values (5x, 10x difference), price will move powerfully in that direction. 

## **7.5 Delta and Candle Colour Relationship** 

- In a green (bullish) candle: buyers were dominant — positive delta expected. 

- In a red (bearish) candle: sellers were dominant — negative delta expected. 

- However, DIVERGENCES between candle colour and delta are highly informative (covered in advanced sessions). 

## **7.6 Delta vs SMC/BMC Candles** 

- SMC (Selling Market Candle) and BMC (Buying Market Candle) are pure order flow patterns — you observe the order flow directly from the candle footprint. 

- Delta is a DERIVED metric — it is the mathematical result of the order flow activity. 

- For experienced, fast traders: use SMC/BMC directly — it is more precise and real-time. 

- For newer or developing traders: use Delta as your primary indicator — it is easier to interpret and still highly effective. 

**Balakrishna's Guidance:** Delta is the byproduct of SMC/PMC candle activity. If you are skilled and active, read the SMC/BMC directly. If you are still developing, rely on delta — it gives you the same directional signal with less complexity.

## **8. Limit vs Aggressive Orders at Support and Resistance** 

This is the most advanced concept covered in this session. It explains how to re-interpret what you see in order flow when price is at — or crossing — a key support or resistance level. 

## **8.1 The Standard Interpretation (Away from S/R)** 

- Under normal market conditions (away from support/resistance zones), the order flow is straightforward: 

- Right-side numbers in a candle = Aggressive Buying (market buy orders). 

- Left-side numbers in a candle = Aggressive Selling (market sell orders). 

- This is the regular, everyday reading of order flow candles. 

## **8.2 The Reversal in Interpretation (At S/R Zones)** 

When a candle is touching or crossing a support or resistance level, the interpretation FLIPS. This is one of the most powerful insights in order flow analysis. 

## **At or Near Resistance** 

- A candle approaches resistance from below — most of the candle body is BELOW the resistance level. 

- This normal activity inside the zone = aggressive buying and aggressive selling (as usual). 

- But the small portion of the candle that CROSSES ABOVE the resistance level? That crossing is caused by Limit Sellers who placed orders specifically at the resistance level. 

- So when a candle pushes briefly above resistance and gets rejected, you are seeing LIMIT SELL orders absorbing the aggressive buyers. 

- Rename: at resistance, 'aggressive buying' becomes an indirect indicator of 'limit selling' at that level. 

## **At or Near Support** 

- A candle approaches support from above — most of the candle body is ABOVE the support level. 

- The small portion of the candle that BREAKS BELOW the support level = caused by Limit Buyers who placed orders at the support level. 

- So when a candle pierces briefly below support and bounces, you are seeing LIMIT BUY orders absorbing aggressive sellers. 

- Rename: at support, 'aggressive selling' becomes an indirect indicator of 'limit buying' at that level.

## **8.3 The King and Queen Analogy** 

Balakrishna explains the relationship between aggressive orders and limit orders with an elegant analogy: 

- The King = Aggressive orders (market orders). These are what you normally observe and interact with in everyday trading — visible, direct, easy to see. 

- The Queen = Limit orders. Equally powerful, perhaps even more so — but not directly visible. You only know the queen is acting when the king is absent (i.e., when aggressive activity stops at a S/R level and price reverses). 

- You transact with the King (aggressive orders) in normal trading. But at key levels, it is the Queen (limit orders) that controls the outcome. 

- When price gets rejected at a resistance: the aggressive buyers (King) ran into the limit sellers (Queen) — and the Queen won. 

**Advanced Insight:** Wherever you see aggressive buying activity at and slightly above a resistance level, understand that it was MADE POSSIBLE by limit sellers sitting at that level. No one can aggressively buy without a limit seller to match with. The resistance was created and held by limit sell orders. 

**📌 Note:** _For now, in most of your analysis, focus on aggressive (market) orders — the King. But always be aware that the S/R levels you are trading are ultimately defined and defended by limit orders — the Queen._

## **10. Glossary of Terms** 

|||
|---|---|
|**Term**|**Definition**|
|||
|||
|**Limit Order**|An order to buy or sell at a specified price. Does not execute until<br>market reaches that price. Also called Passive Order.|
|||
|||
|**Market Order**|An order to buy or sell immediately at the best available price. Also<br>called Aggressive Order. Moves the market.|
|||
|||
|**LTP**|Last Transaction Price — the price at which the most recent trade<br>executed. This is what you see as 'current price'.|
|||
|||
|**Passive Order**|Another name for Limit Order. Waits passively for price to reach it.|
|||
|||
|**Aggressive Order**|Another name for Market Order. Actively hunts for the nearest<br>available counterparty.|
|||
|||
|**Imbalance**|A significant mismatch between buying and selling volume at a<br>specific price level within a candle. Set to 300% threshold.|
|||
|||
|**Imbalance Threshold**|The minimum ratio of buyer-to-seller (or seller-to-buyer) volume<br>required to register as a valid imbalance signal. Use 300%.|
|||
|||
|**Law of Gravity**|Price naturally drifts downward even without active sellers, purely due<br>to lack of new buying demand.|
|||
|||
|**Pullback**|A slow, mild, low-volume retracement within an ongoing trend. Caused<br>by profit booking and lack of new buyers.|
|||
|||
|**Sell-Off**|A fast, sharp, high-volume decline driven by aggressive selling. Not a<br>pullback — do not buy into it.|
|||
|||
|**Retracement**|A temporary price movement against the trend direction. A pullback is<br>a type of mild retracement.|
|||
|||
|**Liquidity**|A price zone where large numbers of both buyers and sellers coexist,<br>creating dense order activity. Acts as support/resistance.|
|||
|||
|**Liquidity Zone**|A price area with high density of orders (usually NRC or WRC). Price<br>is rejected or absorbed here.|
|||
|||
|**Rejection**|Price hits a liquidity zone and bounces away — the zone's orders<br>absorb the incoming pressure and reverse the move.|
|||
|||
|**Delta**|Buy Volume minus Sell Volume. Measures net dominance of buyers<br>vs sellers in a candle.|
|||
|||
|**Positive Delta**|Delta > 0. Buyers are dominant. Buyers exceed sellers by the delta<br>amount.|
|||
|||
|**Negative Delta**|Delta < 0. Sellers are dominant. Sellers exceed buyers by the absolute<br>delta amount.|
|||
|||
|**Dominance**|The state when one side (buyers or sellers) significantly outweighs the<br>other, typically by 100% or more.|
